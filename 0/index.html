Part 1: Selfie - The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

Here are the photos I took for my friend in different distances. I zoomed in while stepping back to make the size of the subject always be the same.

10cm
30cm
80cm
200cm

After comparing these photos, I found that the photo taken at 10cm has significant perspective distortion, making his nose appear disproportionately large compared to the rest of his face. As the distance increases to 30cm and 80cm, the distortion lessens, and facial features appear more balanced and natural. By 200cm, the perspective distortion is minimal, resulting in a more natural representation of him.

This experiment shows that the difference of distance can greatly impact what subject looks like in the photo, even the subject has almost the same size in each photo. Too near distance may cause the people or objects in the photo to look weird and unnatural, while a moderate distance helps get a more flattering and realistic photo.

The Reason Why Distance Matters

The reason why distance matters in photography is due to the principles of perspective. When you take a photo from a close distance, the subject's features are exaggerated. That means the depth difference inside an object may be significant in the overall small distance to the camera. This is particularly noticeable in portraits, where the nose and forehead may appear larger than they are in reality. As you increase the distance, the perspective becomes more natural, and the subject's features are represented more accurately.

As what is showing in these graphs, the inside square in the first graph is much more bigger than the second graph. This definitely illustrates when keeping the size of the whole object same, distance can affect the contrast between protruding and recessed parts inside an object, resulting in different photos.

Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

The First Shot (From Far Away, Zoomed In):

The scene appears compressed or flattened. The buildings and objects along the street seem stacked on top of each other, closer together than they are in reality. There's a sense of density and layers. This is the "perspective compression" effect often used in cityscape and architectural photography.

The Second Shot (Up Close, Wide Angle):

The scene has exaggerated depth and expansive perspective. The building down the street look very far away and small. The distance between objects feels vast and stretched out.

Why This Happens

The compression effect happens because from a great distance, just like what happens in part 1, the relative space between objects like buildings becomes a tiny fraction of their vast distance from the camera, making them appear stacked closely together. When you zoom in from that far position, you isolate this flattened perspective. In contrast, walking close to the scene drastically exaggerates the perceived space between the foreground and background, making the same street look much longer and deeper.
